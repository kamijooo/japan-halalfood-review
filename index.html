Halal Omakase in Tokyo —
I Finally Found the Real Thing

Anna K. Travel Blogger · August 2025 · Shinjuku, Tokyo
📍 omakase 墨 — Shinjuku, Tokyo. The counter seat I'd been dreaming about.

I need to write this down before the feeling fades. I'm on the train home, still full, still kind of stunned.

I spent three days in Shinjuku looking for one good halal meal. Not just "halal-friendly" — I mean an actual, proper, sit-down experience. Counter seating. Chef interaction. The full thing.

I walked past so many beautiful restaurants. Peeked at menus, asked questions, got vague answers. Most places couldn't confirm what was in the broth. One spot used mirin in the sauce. Another just felt like it was designed for tourists who don't know any better.

Then I found omakase 墨.

✦
This is me right after being seated at the counter. My friend took this photo. I was trying to look calm. I was not calm. 📷 iPhone 14 · omakase 墨, Shinjuku · August 2025
The Arrival

A Quiet Street in Shinjuku. Very Understated Door.

The restaurant is about 5 minutes from Shinjuku Station. Easy to find, but you'd walk past if you weren't looking — just a small wooden door with warm light, no flashy signage.

We were seated almost immediately. Eight counter seats, maybe five guests that evening. Quiet. Intimate. Exactly right. The chef came over, introduced himself, and asked about preferences beyond our halal requirements. That small gesture told me everything.

📸 Pale wood, low lighting, clean lines. You can see everything the chef does from the counter.
The Food · Sushi

The Rice Stopped Me Mid-Bite

I expected the fish to be the star. It was — but the rice got me first. Body-temperature warm. Subtly vinegared. The kind of thing you don't notice until it's missing everywhere else you've eaten.

Toro nigiri draped like silk. Uni that was creamy and sweet — not the aggressively oceanic kind. By the second course, my friend had put her hand on my arm and said "we're coming back."

We'd been seated for 20 minutes.

Toro nigiri. The chef's hands — I watched every single cut.

"That uni was the best single bite I've put in my mouth. I said what I said."

The Food · Wagyu

He Brought Out a Charcoal Grill Tableside. I Closed My Eyes.

Midway through, the chef set a small binchotan grill in front of us. The smell. I just... closed my eyes for a second.

A5 Wagyu. Three slices. 90 seconds over the coals. Flaky salt. A little yuzu.

This place serves ultra-rare lean Wagyu — the kind that makes up maybe 5% of the market. Not the overly fatty stuff. The owner is fifth-generation Wagyu merchant, sources directly from farms, visits them himself. You can taste the difference. It's not just richness — there's actual depth, actual flavor.

My friend and I didn't say anything for a while after finishing.

📸 This was my actual plate. Lean wagyu — the marbling is there, but it's not overwhelming.

"This is what wagyu actually tastes like. Everything I had before was a copy of a copy."

🌙 Halal Certified

For Muslim Travelers — What You Actually Need to Know

I know. This is the section you've been scrolling for. Here's everything, clearly:

  • No pork or pork-derived ingredients anywhere in the kitchen
  • No alcohol — not in cooking, not in sauces, not anywhere
  • Wagyu sourced from halal-certified slaughterhouses in Japan
  • Dashi broth is kombu-based (no bonito, no mirin) for halal guests
  • Halal certification visible at the entrance — I verified it myself
  • English-speaking staff — no language barrier anxiety
🕌 Prayer room on-site. You don't need to leave between courses.
My Honest Take

"I didn't expect it to be this good."

I mean that in the best way. Some part of me had quietly prepared for "good considering the halal constraint." That's not what happened. This was just good. Full stop.

The food, the pacing, the chef's attention, the quiet of the counter — all of it together felt like something that had been thought about for a very long time.

I'd recommend this to literally anyone visiting Tokyo who wants one really memorable meal. Muslim or not.
