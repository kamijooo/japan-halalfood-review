I Tried a Halal Omakase in Tokyo
— Wagyu & Sushi That Blew My Mind

Anna K. ✈️

📍 The omakase counter — I couldn't believe I'd actually found this
Introduction

Okay so I have to tell you about this meal. Like, I'm still thinking about it weeks later, and that NEVER happens to me.

I was in Tokyo for two weeks this spring — my third time visiting Japan, but the first time I was really committed to finding proper fine dining that I could actually eat as a Muslim traveler. No pork, no alcohol, no compromises. Just incredible food.

And let me tell you... Tokyo does not make this easy. I'd spent three frustrating evenings just walking past beautiful restaurants, peeking in at menus, and sighing. Most omakase places either couldn't confirm halal, or the chef's sauce contained mirin, or the vibe just wasn't right.

Then my friend Zara (she runs a food account, follow her seriously) sent me a recommendation for a place in Ginza. She said: "Just trust me. Book it."

✦
The Struggle Was Real

Finding Halal Fine Dining in Tokyo... is Hard

I want to be real with you here because I've seen so many blog posts that make it sound easy. It's not.

The standard tourist restaurant recommendations online are full of places that either: a) can't tell you what's in the broth, b) use alcohol in marinades, or c) are just... mediocre tourist traps dressed up nicely.

If you want the real experience — counter seating, chef's selection, fresh seasonal ingredients — that's genuinely hard to access as a halal-conscious diner in Japan.

"I didn't come all the way to Tokyo to eat the same chicken rice I could've had at home."

I wanted the actual experience. Wagyu. Omakase sushi. A chef who actually cares. I just needed somewhere that could guarantee it was prepared right.

The Discovery

I Found It — Here's How

Zara's DM came through on a Wednesday. She'd eaten there two months prior and said the chef was trained at a Michelin-starred kitchen. The restaurant specializes in omakase for international guests, with a specifically designed halal menu — not a watered-down version, but genuinely crafted around what's possible without alcohol or pork.

I looked it up. Read the reviews. Saw the photos. The counter was immaculate. The wagyu photos were... honestly obscene. I booked a table for two for Friday night.

The Visit · Part 1

Arriving in Ginza on a Friday Night

Getting there was genuinely easy — which I appreciated because Tokyo's side streets can be a maze. The restaurant is on a quiet street off Ginza's main drag, tucked between a gallery and a flower shop. Very understated entrance. No flashy signage. Just a small wooden door with a single light above it.

📸 The street approaching the restaurant — I almost walked past it twice

We were met at the door and seated almost immediately. Eight counter seats total. That night there were maybe five guests — it felt intimate and quiet in a way that instantly put me at ease.

The Visit · Part 2

First Impression: "Oh. This is the real thing."

The interior is minimal in that very specifically Japanese way — pale wood, low lighting, clean lines. Nothing trying too hard. The counter wraps in a gentle curve around the kitchen, and you can see everything: the fish being broken down, the coals being adjusted, the knives being wiped between cuts.

We got seated right at the counter — this was my friend's face approximately two seconds after we sat down. Same, honestly. 📷 Photo by me, iPhone 14 — no filters, just good lighting

The chef came over, introduced himself, and asked if either of us had any preferences or dislikes beyond our halal requirements. A small thing, but it mattered. It felt like a conversation, not a transaction.

✦
The Food · Sushi Course

The Sushi — Course by Course

We started with four pieces of nigiri. I don't want to oversell this, but the rice alone was a revelation. It was warm — body temperature warm — and seasoned with a vinegar blend that was subtle but present. The tuna was fatty toro, draped like silk.

Left: toro nigiri. Right: chef's hands at work — I watched every single cut.

The sea urchin course came mid-way through. I've had uni before that tasted like the ocean had an argument. This was the opposite — creamy, sweet, practically melting. My friend literally put her hand on my arm and said "we're coming back."

"That uni was the single best bite of food I have ever put in my mouth. I said what I said."

The Food · Wagyu Course

The Wagyu — I Need a Moment

Halfway through the meal, the chef brought out a small charcoal grill. The smell alone was... I'm not a dramatic person, but I closed my eyes.

A5 wagyu. Three thin slices. Grilled tableside over binchotan charcoal for about 90 seconds. Served with a pinch of flaky salt and a small dish of citrus.

📸 This was my actual plate. The marbling — I can still see it when I close my eyes.

The fat-to-meat ratio meant it basically dissolved before you finished chewing. It sounds like a cliché but it is, genuinely, what happened. My friend and I just looked at each other and didn't say anything for a while.

The portion felt small at first — three slices — but by the time it was done, I understood. Any more would've been indulgent in a way that would have overwhelmed the other courses. The chef knows what he's doing.

🌙 HALAL CERTIFIED

For My Fellow Muslim Travelers — Here's What You Need to Know

I know this is the section you've been waiting for. So let me be clear and direct:

  • No pork or pork-derived products — none, anywhere in the kitchen
  • No alcohol — not in cooking sauces, not in marinades, not anywhere
  • The wagyu is sourced from halal-certified suppliers
  • Staff are trained to answer halal-related questions clearly
  • Halal certification is visible at the entrance (I checked)
  • English-speaking staff available — no language barrier stress

I asked about the dashi stock (common issue with Japanese food — often contains bonito or mirin). They use a kombu-based dashi for halal guests. Honestly, I couldn't tell any difference in taste — it was still deeply umami and rich.

My Honest Reaction

"I didn't expect it to be this good."

And I mean that in the best way. I think there's a part of me that had mentally prepared for "good for halal" — like, adjusted expectations based on constraints. That is not what happened.

This was just... good. Full stop. The kind of meal I'd recommend to literally anyone visiting Tokyo — Muslim or not — who wants a genuinely exceptional food experience.

The chef's attention, the quality of the ingredients, the pacing of the courses, the quiet intimacy of the counter — all of it together felt like something that had been thought about very carefully for a very long time.
